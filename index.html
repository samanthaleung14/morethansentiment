About the Archive

Music functions as cultural memory and emotional record — a vessel for identity, protest, nostalgia, and longing. But artificial intelligence is changing how music is made, circulated, and trusted, creating new and urgent challenges for archives.

This project is organized into two movements. The first examines human-made music as a record of feeling across contexts: heartbreak, bravado, social critique, resilience, and longing. The second confronts the problem of unstable authorship — AI-generated or AI-altered music where audiences can no longer easily distinguish what is real, original, or human-made.

Each artifact carries a museum-style entry connecting creator, year, cultural circulation, and rhetorical function. Together they build a curated argument: that AI tools, however powerful, consistently miss what makes music matter. Click any artifact card to explore the full analysis.

Beauty and a Beat
Justin Bieber feat. Nicki Minaj
2012 · Believe · RBMG / Def Jam
Produced by Max Martin, Zedd
classificationenergynostalgiaslangpersonaBART MNLIJaccard 0.60

Museum Entry

"Beauty and a Beat" is a nostalgic, high-energy pop/rap collaboration that most of its generation knows by heart. The lyrics often portray party-like imagery and are oftentimes repetitive, creating immersive, high-energy listening experiences. This pop/rap song is versatile as it creates persona and rhythm that provide meaning and symbolism within the music. However, its power comes almost entirely from its production, cultural context, and delivery rather than its lyrics in isolation — making it an ideal stress test for AI classification tools that rely on lyrical pattern-matching.

BART Large MNLI — Zero-Shot Classification
Method: Zero-shot classification via BART Large MNLI · Metric: Jaccard Similarity (manual vs. AI tags)

We used BART Large MNLI to classify dissected lyrics from "Beauty and a Beat." This model assigns text to categories without being explicitly trained on a specific dataset, making it useful for archival tagging workflows. We compared AI-generated labels side-by-side with manually identified thematic categories.

In our manual analysis, the primary themes were nightlife/party, dancing/music, and romance — categories reflecting the song's target demographics: club environments, high-energy social settings, and intimate interactions between Justin Bieber and Nicki Minaj's listeners.

The model privileges frequently repeated vocabulary over broader cultural context, missing the significance of performer identity as a thematic signal.

Archival Verdict

BART Large MNLI is a useful tool for first-pass metadata generation on large lyric datasets — an archive holding thousands of songs can generate thematic labels to assist with research and discovery. However, because it relies primarily on lyrical patterns, it overlooks contextual and cultural meaning. AI-generated labels should undergo human review, especially for content where cultural significance is carried by performer identity rather than lyrical frequency. Not ready for fully automated archival tagging.

Artifact 02 · Sentiment Analysis
Royals
Lorde
2013 · Pure Heroine · Universal Music NZ
Written by Ella Yelich-O'Connor & Joel Little
sentimentsatireironyVADERGemini 2.5social critiquewealth

Museum Entry

"Royals" is a flagship example of pop that uses a catchy, confident tone to deliver satire and social critique about wealth and status. The song sets up an "us vs. them" worldview that is less about envy and more about refusal — and that's exactly the kind of nuance that gets flattened when AI forces songs into simple positive/negative buckets. The song is self-aware about performance and status, even while being a mainstream pop product itself.

"We don't care, we aren't caught up in your love affair / And we'll never be royals."

Analysis
Gemini 2.5 Flash vs. VADER — Irony Detection
Method: 10 lyric excerpts rated by AI vs. human · Metric: Success rate, VADER compound score (–1 to 1)

We compared two AI sentiment tools — Google's Gemini 2.5 Flash (an LLM) and NLTK's VADER (a rule-based lexicon model) — against human-labeled sentiment ratings for ten lyric excerpts from "Royals." Gemini achieved a success rate of 50% and VADER scored 40%.

Both tools struggled with the same core problem: "Royals" is built on irony and satire, and neither could detect when language that sounds positive is actually being used critically. The excerpt listing luxury brand names — "Cristal, Maybach, diamonds on your timepiece, jet planes, islands, tigers on a gold leash" — was labeled negative by the human rater because the song mocks that excess. Both tools rated it positive, reading glamorous vocabulary at face value.

VADER even rated the "bloodstains, ball gowns" excerpt as positive because words like "gold" triggered positive associations in its lexicon. The tools only succeeded when surface-level language aligned with actual intent.

"Cristal, Maybach, diamonds on your timepiece, jet planes…"Positive (luxury vocab)Negative — satirical mockery of excess

VADER's score of 0.9919 is especially telling. VADER sees "queen," "rule," "great," and "diamonds" without any mechanism for understanding irony.

Archival Verdict

For archival use, this tool produces a workable first draft but not a reliable finished product. An archivist using this transcript for search, tagging, or metadata would be working with a version of the song where roughly a quarter of the content is wrong, which could lead to missed themes, broken keyword searches, and misrepresented meaning. Guardrails should include mandatory human review for any content with rapid or non-standard speech patterns, and ideally a side-by-side comparison workflow where the AI draft is corrected against the audio rather than trusted on its own.

Artifact 03 · Transcription Analysis
Rap God
Eminem
2013 · The Marshall Mathers LP 2 · Aftermath / Interscope
transcriptionWER 92.2%Whisperspeedperformancelegibilitymetadata
Listen on YouTube
Museum Entry

"Rap God" is a six-minute technical showcase where Eminem delivers some of the fastest sustained vocal passages in mainstream rap history, peaking at roughly 11.4 syllables per second. The track is packed with wordplay, cultural references, and breathless transitions — easy for a human listener with context to follow, but a serious challenge for automated transcription. We ran the track through OpenAI's Whisper and got a Word Error Rate of 24.68%, meaning one in four words was wrong. That is good enough to capture the general shape of the lyrics, but in a song where every syllable is deliberate, losing a quarter of the content means losing punchlines, references, and the precision that is the entire point of the performance. "Rap God" shows that even when AI transcription mostly works, "mostly" is not enough for an archive where accuracy determines what future audiences can find.

OBS Recorded Clip — A short excerpt
Analysis
OpenAI Whisper — Word Error Rate Analysis
Method: AI transcript (Whisper) vs. authoritative manual transcript · Metric: Word Error Rate (WER)

We used OpenAI's Whisper model to generate an AI transcript of "Rap God" and compared it against an authoritative manual transcript using Word Error Rate (WER) as the quantitative measure.

Word Error Rate92.2%errors per 100 words
Manual Transcript1,564words (authoritative)
AI Output291words (Whisper)

The results were striking. A WER of 92.2% means that for every 100 words in the authoritative transcript, roughly 92 contained some form of error. The Whisper output contained only 291 words against 1,564 in the manual — the model failed to capture the vast majority of spoken content. A closer look revealed the problem went beyond simple mishearings: the output included processing metadata like timestamps and system strings rather than actual lyrics, meaning the raw output was never properly post-processed.

Even beyond the metadata issue, the dramatic word-count gap suggests the model could not keep up with Eminem's rapid-fire delivery — clocking upwards of 6 syllables per second in peak sections.

Archival Verdict

Transcription is not a neutral mechanical step — it is a gatekeeper for what parts of a musical artifact become legible to future audiences. This tool is clearly not ready for performance-heavy music without significant human oversight. Archives working with rap or spoken word must treat AI transcription as a rough first pass only. Guardrails: automatically flag when AI transcript word count is dramatically shorter than expected; mandate human review for rapid or non-standard speech patterns. Not ready as a standalone archival tool.

Analysis
Tokenization, Keyword Extraction & Bigram Analysis
Method A: Tokenization + stopword removal + word frequency (tidytext, R) · Method B: N-gram (bigram) extraction

To analyze the excerpt from "Alright," we used two NLP techniques: keyword extraction and phrase-level theme extraction. Using the tidytext package in R, we tokenized the lyric excerpt into individual words, removed stopwords, and counted word frequency to identify salient terms. We then ran bigram extraction to identify recurring phrases signaling the artist's themes.

Keyword Analysis: Themes of conflict, vulnerability, and resistance dominated the vocabulary. Words like hurt, kill, hate, gun, dead pointed to a lexical field of violence and threat. Terms like pride, weak, world suggested emotional struggle and disillusionment. The repetition of po — slang for police — signals institutional authority and police brutality as a central concern.

Bigram Analysis: N-gram extraction highlighted phrases: "wanna kill," "po po," "hate po," and "getting weak." These phrases share a common denominator of danger and physical vulnerability, allowing us to infer the artist's narrative about police brutality.

Archival Verdict

NLP techniques can surface thematic motifs and serve as useful exploratory scaffolding. However, they cannot register the rhetorical function of words within a cultural tradition. The defiance encoded in "we gon' be alright" is not accessible to a tool that lacks the cultural knowledge to read it as resistance rather than optimism. Human review is essential for protest, religious, or culturally embedded content. Useful first-pass tool; not a substitute for culturally informed human analysis.

Analysis I — Translation
Google Translate vs. Human — Puerto Rican Slang
Method: 8 lines with slang/colloquial phrasing · Metric: SBERT cosine similarity (Google output vs. human translation)

We selected eight lines from "DTMF" containing Puerto Rican slang, colloquial contractions, or culturally specific phrasing and compared Google Translate's Spanish-to-English output against a human translation using SBERT cosine similarity. The Google↔Human similarity score tells us how close the machine translation came to a culturally informed human reading.

The results showed a sharp divide. Straightforward lines scored extremely high — "Otro sunset bonito que veo en San Juan" hit 0.9968, and "Aquí en la isla e' donde yo quiero estar" scored a perfect 1.0. But slang-heavy lines told a different story.

The average of 0.8442 looks decent at first glance. But it masks the real problem: reliability swings from near-perfect on standard lines to barely above chance on slang. The lines where translation fails hardest are exactly the lines that carry the most cultural specificity — the slang, contractions, and embedded social references that make this a Puerto Rican song rather than generic pop.

Archival Verdict

Average scores alone are insufficient — variance across lines matters as much as the mean. Not ready for culturally specific multilingual archival metadata without human review.

Artifact 06 · Sentiment Analysis
Dancing Queen
ABBA
1976 · ABBA Gold · Polar Music
Written by Benny Andersson, Stig Anderson, Björn Ulvaeus
sentimentBERTVADERcontextjoytransformermethodology
Search on YouTube
Museum Entry

"Dancing Queen" is one of the most immediately recognizable expressions of joy in the pop canon — a song whose positivity seems self-evident to any listener. Yet it serves as a revealing test case for sentiment analysis methodology, because the lyrics use mostly neutral vocabulary ("queen," "girl," "dance") to express a deeply positive emotional experience. This makes it an ideal artifact for comparing lexicon-based and context-aware AI sentiment models side by side.

OBS Recording Clip
OBS Recorded Clip — A short excerpt
Analysis
VADER vs. BERT — The Contextual Sentiment Gap
Method A: VADER lexicon scoring · Method B: BERT transformer model · 9 lyric lines analyzed

To assess the emotional tone of "Dancing Queen," we used two sentiment analysis methods with fundamentally different architectures. The comparison reveals a stark methodological split between lexicon-based and contextual approaches.

VADER gave an average sentiment score of approximately 0.08 — slightly above neutral. Although these lyrics obviously convey positive sentiment to human ears, VADER cannot provide an accurate analysis because it assigns scores to individual words based on a predefined lexicon. Many words in the excerpt — "queen," "girl," "dance" — carry neutral lexicon values. VADER cannot recognize them as emotionally positive because it does not consider the context or overall sentence structure.

BERT produced a markedly different result, classifying 8 of 9 lines as extremely positive (≈0.99). Unlike VADER, BERT's transformer architecture evaluates sentiment based on the full context and overall meaning of each sentence. Phrases like "having the time of your life" and "you can dance" are correctly identified as strongly positive because BERT understands their relational and contextual meaning.

BERT Score≈0.998 of 9 lines: extremely +
Human RatingHigh+clearly joyful / celebratory
Lyric LineVADER ScoreBERT ScoreHuman Rating

The comparison highlights a critical methodological insight. Lexicon-based sentiment analysis struggles with lyrics because normally neutral words cannot be overturned and assessed as positive or negative in context. Contextual transformer models are better at capturing the narrative as a whole and perform a more accurate analysis of the author's intended message.

Archival Verdict

For archival sentiment metadata, BERT-class models represent a significant improvement over lexicon-based tools like VADER — particularly for music, where emotional meaning is encoded in phrasing and context rather than individual word sentiment. However, even BERT requires human oversight for irony, satire, and culturally specific emotional registers (as demonstrated by "Royals"). The choice of sentiment tool should be documented as part of archive metadata provenance. BERT conditionally ready; VADER requires supplementation with human review.
